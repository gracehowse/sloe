## 0. Why this memo exists (the Suppr lesson)

The placeholder name **"Suppr"** was adopted without a formal clearance step and
carries an **unresolved HIGH phonetic-collision risk** with the live App Store
competitor **"Supper Club!"** (same lane: food). That risk is *load-bearing* — it
is one of the active reasons a rebrand is wanted at all.

The whole point of this memo is to **not repeat that mistake** for "Sloe": do the
public-record screen first, name the residual risk honestly, and hand counsel a
precise brief before a single dollar is spent on rebrand execution. A preliminary
screen that returns "looks fine" without a register pull is exactly the false
comfort that let Suppr through. This memo therefore treats the **outstanding
register pull as the gate**, not the searches we *could* run.

## 1. Summary verdict

**Preliminary risk rating: MEDIUM.**
**Recommendation: GO — pending attorney clearance.** Proceed to a full attorney
register search and grab the defensive assets now; do **not** announce, file, or
commit the rebrand until counsel confirms class 9 in UK + EU + US.

Why MEDIUM and not Low:
- **The good news (why not High):** repeated App Store / web searches found **no
  recipe, nutrition, calorie, meal-planner, or food-tracking app named "Sloe."**
  The specific failure mode that sinks Suppr — a same-category, same-channel
  (App Store) collision — does **not** appear to exist for Sloe. That is the
  single most important positive finding.
- **Why not Low:** "sloe" is a **common dictionary word** (the blackthorn berry)
  with heavy commercial use in the drinks space ("sloe gin"), a live cosmetics
  namesake (**Sloé**, accented), and an unsearched register. A common word in a
  food-adjacent field yields at best a **narrow, weak mark with likely
  coexistence** — not exclusivity. And the **definitive register pull in class 9
  is still outstanding** (all registers blocked our automated access). We cannot
  responsibly call a name "Low risk" when the one search that proves it is the
  one we have not run.

MEDIUM means: very likely adoptable and defensible *as a brand*, probably
registrable in class 9, but expect a **narrow registration**, expect to
**coexist** rather than to exclude, and **confirm class 9 is clear before commit.**

## 2. Risk by trademark class

"Sloe" is strong or weak depending entirely on the **distance between the word and
the goods**. The further "sloe" sits from an actual blackthorn product, the more
*arbitrary* — and the more protectable — it becomes.

|---|---|---|---|---|
| **9 — PRIMARY** | Downloadable software; mobile apps; recipe/nutrition software | **Arbitrary → strong.** A blackthorn berry has nothing to do with software, so "Sloe" is inherently distinctive in class 9, like "Apple" for computers. This is where our mark lives and where it is most ownable. | **Low–Medium** | Best-case class. Strength here is real *if* the register is clear. The whole filing strategy should centre on class 9. Outstanding: register pull. |
| **5** | Dietary/nutritional supplements; medical/nutritional preparations | **Suggestive at most; not descriptive.** We don't sell supplements, so exposure here is low *for our goods*, but third-party "sloe"/"sloe berry" supplement marks (the berry is a real botanical ingredient) may exist and crowd the class. | **Medium** | We likely **don't need class 5** (we're a tool, not a supplement — and the trust posture forbids health claims). Filing here invites avoidable conflicts. Recommend **not** filing class 5 unless counsel sees a defensive reason. |
| **29 / 30** | Foodstuffs (29: preserves/jams; 30: staples, condiments) | **Descriptive / weak.** "Sloe" *is* a food ingredient — sloe jam, sloe preserves exist. Don't file 43. Same as above: relevant only as crowding/coexistence context, not as our own exposure. |

**Class takeaway:** file **class 9** (and only class 9 unless counsel advises
otherwise). Sloe is **strong where we need it (software)** and **weak where we
don't (food/drink)**. The weakness in 29/30/43 is not our problem *unless* we
overreach the application into those goods — so keep the spec tight to software,
recipe/nutrition functionality, and downloadable apps.

## 3. Likelihood-of-confusion analysis

Likelihood of confusion turns on (a) similarity of the marks and (b) relatedness
of the goods/channels. Run the three live threads:

**(a) "Sloé" — French eco-cosmetics, est. 2019.**
- Mark: "Sloé" (**accented é**), Nice **class 3** (cosmetics), a different field,
  a different country of origin, and a **different spelling**.
- Goods are **unrelated** to software/recipes; trade channels don't overlap
  (a cosmetics brand vs an iOS recipe app). The accent further distinguishes the
  marks on the register.
- **Preliminary read: coexistence realistic, low conflict.** Class 3 vs class 9,
  cosmetics vs software — this is textbook non-overlapping. The residual risk is
  brand-confusion in *search* (a consumer Googling "sloe" finds both), which is a
  marketing/SEO issue, not a registrability blocker. Counsel should still confirm
  Sloé holds no class-9 registration or EU/UK extension.

**(b) "Sloe gin" / drinks marks.**
- "Sloe gin" is a **generic descriptor of a liquor category**, not a single
  brand — so it does not itself block anything. But individual drinks brands using
  "Sloe" in classes 32/33 (and possibly 43) will exist.
- Goods (alcoholic drinks / bar services) are **unrelated** to a recipe-tracking
  app. **Low confusion risk to our class-9 use.** Relevant only as evidence that
  "sloe" is a *crowded, common term* in the consumables world — which reinforces
  that our mark will be **narrow**, and that we should not stray into food/drink
  classes where this crowd lives.

**(c) The common-word problem.**
- This is the real story. "Sloe" is an everyday English word. Common dictionary
  words can absolutely function as trademarks **when arbitrary to the goods**
  (Apple, Orange, Sage-as-was) — and in class 9 "sloe" *is* arbitrary. But a
  common word also means:
  - **Many unrelated holders** across many classes (cosmetics, drinks,
    hospitality, possibly more once the register is pulled);
  - **A narrower scope of protection** — we could likely register and defend
    "Sloe" *for recipe/nutrition software*, but we would **not** be able to stop a
    sloe-gin brand, a cosmetics line, or a café from using "sloe" in their lanes;
  - **Coexistence is the expected outcome, not the exception.**

**Is coexistence realistic? Yes — and it is the plan.** We are not seeking a
worldwide monopoly on the word "sloe"; we are seeking a defensible class-9 mark
for an iOS recipe/nutrition app. On the public record so far, that is realistic:
no in-lane app collision, the one same-spelling-ish brand (Sloé) sits in an
unrelated class with an accent, and the drinks/food uses are in classes we won't
touch. **The mark is narrow but ownable in our lane** — confirmed only by the
register pull.

## 4. The "slow" homophone — legal / ® implications

The marketing team treats /sləʊ/ = "slow" as a **feature** (calm, unhurried,
the brand's whole posture, the `eatsloe.com`/`sloe.life` puns). That is fine for
positioning. The **legal** angle is separate and is a genuine risk-widener:

- **Phonetic equivalence is a recognised confusion factor.** Trademark offices and
  courts weigh *sound* alongside spelling and meaning. "Sloe" and "slow" are
  **aural identicals**. This is structurally the **same kind of risk that sinks
  Suppr** — Suppr's HIGH rating comes precisely from phonetic equivalence with
  "Supper." We should be honest that we are adopting another homophone name.
- **Practical effect:** the confusion net for "Sloe" is **broader than its
  spelling** — it can sweep in **"Slow"-named marks** in related classes. A
  "Slow" or "Slo" food/recipe/wellness app, brand, or service (and the
  slow-food movement is a real, branded space) could be cited against us, or
  could object to us, on phonetic grounds even though we spell it differently.
- **Mitigation / why this is more survivable than Suppr's case:**
  1. Unlike Suppr-vs-Supper (both squarely in *food*, both on the App Store), any
     "Slow" conflict would still need **related goods** to bite. A slow-fashion or
     slow-tech "Slow" brand doesn't threaten a class-9 recipe app.
  2. The **specific, named, in-lane collision** that makes Suppr HIGH
     ("Supper Club!", a live food app) **has no observed equivalent for Sloe** —
     no "Slow" *recipe/nutrition app* surfaced. The danger is theoretical until
     the register pull finds (or doesn't find) a "Slow" mark in class 9 / 5 /
     43.
  3. The accent-free, berry-anchored spelling and the always-with-the-mark
     wordmark guidance reduce *marketplace* confusion (a separate issue from
     registrability).
- **Action for counsel:** the register search **must explicitly include "SLOW",
  "SLO", and "SLOE" as phonetic variants** in class 9 (and 5/43). Do not search
  the literal spelling only — that is the trap that would let a homophone
  conflict through exactly as it did for Suppr.

**Net:** the homophone is a marketing asset and a **legal risk-widener**. It does
not make Sloe High on its own (no in-lane "Slow" app found), but it **raises the
bar for the search** — phonetic variants are mandatory, not optional.

## 5. Jurisdiction plan

Three offices matter: **UK-IPO** (home market), **EUIPO** (EU coverage), **USPTO**
(US — the App Store's home market and the largest viral-reach surface). Sequencing,
given a UK home market + US App Store + an EU presence + a ~2026-07-01 viral launch:

**Recommended filing order and rationale:**

1. **File UK-IPO first.** It is the home market, the cheapest and fastest office,
   and establishes a clear first national right and priority date. A UK class-9
   application is the anchor.
2. **Use the UK filing as a Paris Convention priority basis for EUIPO and USPTO
   within 6 months.** Filing UK first then claiming priority gives all three the
   **same effective date** while spreading cost and letting the (cheap, fast) UK
   examination surface problems before the (pricier) EU/US filings commit.
3. **EUIPO** — one application covers all EU member states; file in parallel or on
   the UK priority. Watch for the **Sloé (FR, class 3)** holder and any EU drinks
   marks; class-9 distance should keep us clear, but EUIPO is where a French
   cosmetics extension would show.
4. **USPTO** — the US is **intent-to-use (1(b))**-friendly, which suits a
   pre-launch name: we can file before first US use and perfect on launch. The US
   also has the **most aggressive common-law (unregistered) trademark regime**, so
   the US **knockout/full search matters most here** — an unregistered "Slow"/"Sloe"
   food or wellness brand can have enforceable common-law rights without any
   register entry. This is the jurisdiction where the homophone risk is most likely
   to surface and where we must search hardest.

**Timing vs the July launch (be realistic):**
- **Registration will NOT complete before 2026-07-01.** UK/EU/US registration takes
  months (examination + opposition windows). That is normal and not a blocker.
- What we **can and must** have before launch: (1) a **clean attorney search**
  confirming no blocking class-9 conflict, and (2) **applications on file** (at least
  UK, ideally all three on priority) establishing our date. **Launching on an
  uncleared name is the Suppr mistake** — *pending registration* is fine, *un-searched*
  is not.
- **Hard gate:** do not announce or commit the rebrand publicly until the **search
  is clean and applications are filed.** Filing is fast; the search is the long pole.

## 7. Explicit limitation

**This is a product-legal preliminary screen, not a clearance.** It was assembled
from public web and App Store searches on 2026-06-02. It is **NOT** a substitute for
a registered trademark attorney's opinion, and it does **not** clear "Sloe" for use
or registration.

The decisive step — a **definitive register pull in the relevant classes
(class 9 primary; 5/29/30/43 for context) across UK-IPO, EUIPO and USPTO,
including phonetic variants** — is **OUTSTANDING**, because every register
(USPTO TESS, EUIPO/TMview, UK-IPO, Trademarkia, Justia) **blocked our automated
access** (403 / not web-indexed). **That register pull is the gate.** Until a
qualified attorney completes it and issues a written clearance, "Sloe" must be
treated as **unverified** and the rebrand must **not** be committed, announced,
or relied upon. Adopting an un-searched name is the precise error that left
"Suppr" with its unresolved HIGH phonetic-collision risk. Do not repeat it.
