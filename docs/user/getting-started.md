# Getting Started with Sloe

Sign up, answer a few quick questions, see your targets, and log your first
meal — this guide walks you through everything that happens the first time
you open Sloe, from the welcome screen to landing on your Today screen with
something real already logged.

## What is Sloe?

Sloe is a macro tracker at its core, with recipes and meal planning designed
to support that, not compete with it. You get a daily calorie and macro
target, a food diary to log against it, and a collection of recipes —
imported from anywhere on the web, or planned into your week — that show
you real nutrition information for every ingredient.

## From welcome screen to your first logged meal

This is the very first thing you'll do in Sloe. It happens once, the first
time you open the app, and ends with you having a real meal logged on
Today — not just a number you were shown.

```
Welcome → (optional: which app are you coming from?) → a few questions
   → your targets, shown to you → create your account
   → (optional: bring your data with you) → log something, right now
   → Today
```

### 1. Welcome

You'll see the Sloe logo on a calm, full-screen welcome screen — no forms,
no menu, just "Get started" and, underneath it, "I already have an account"
for anyone returning. A small line at the bottom ("Private by default ·
About a minute") sets expectations before you commit to anything.

**Why this step exists:** first impressions matter more than any single
feature — this is a deliberate breath before the questionnaire starts.

**What happens next:** tapping "Get started" takes you into the
questionnaire. "I already have an account" sends you to sign-in instead (see
"Signing back in" below).

### 2. "Coming from another app?" (you might not see this)

Some people see an optional screen asking which tracking app they're
switching from — MyFitnessPal, Lose It!, Cronometer, MacroFactor, or none
of these. Not everyone sees this yet, so you might go straight to the
questions instead — either way is completely normal.

**Why this step exists:** if you've been tracking for years in another app,
Sloe wants to point you toward the right way to bring that history in later
in the flow, rather than leaving you to figure it out yourself.

**What happens next:** straight into the questions, either way.

### 3. A few questions

A short questionnaire builds your nutrition profile: your goal, your body
stats (sex, age, height, weight — or you can skip the scale entirely, see
below), your activity level, how quickly you want to move toward your goal,
any dietary restrictions, and — if you want to override the default — your
own macro split.

**Why this step exists:** every number Sloe shows you afterwards (calorie
target, protein/carb/fat targets, what a recipe "fits" your day) is
calculated from these answers.

**Skipping the scale:** if you'd rather not enter a starting weight, you
can — Sloe will work out your targets from what you log instead of asking
for a number up front. This also skips the pace question, since pace is
measured in weight change per week.

**If your chosen pace is quite aggressive:** you'll see a gentle warning
instead of being blocked outright — for most warnings, tapping past it once
is enough to continue. For very large calorie deficits, you'll be asked to
confirm you understand the numbers before moving on. Sloe won't stop you,
but it won't let an unsafe number slip through unnoticed either.

**What happens next:** your targets.

### 4. Seeing your targets

On the web app, Sloe shows you your calculated calorie and macro targets
right here, before asking you to create an account — the idea is that you
should see the value first, then decide to sign up.

On the iPhone app, this currently works a little differently: you're asked
to sign in first, and you see your targets afterwards. We're working on
bringing the two experiences closer together over time, so if what you see
on iPhone doesn't match this page, that's expected — not a mistake.

**If you skipped the scale in step 3:** instead of concrete numbers, you'll
see a message explaining that your targets will be worked out from your
first week or two of logging.

**What happens next:** creating your account.

### 5. Creating your account

This is where the two platforms genuinely differ, on purpose:

- **On the web app**, you create an account with your first name, email,
  and a password, plus agreeing to the Terms and Privacy Policy. You'll get
  a "check your email" confirmation step.
- **On the iPhone app**, you sign up with **Apple Sign-In only** — there's
  no email/password form during sign-up. This is an Apple App Store
  requirement, not a Sloe choice.

Either way, you need a real, working account before you can continue —
Sloe won't let you past this step on a half-finished sign-up.

**What happens next:** an optional step for bringing in data from
elsewhere.

### 6. Bring your data with you (optional)

A short, skippable step offering a few ways to carry existing data into
Sloe: paste in your current calorie or macro targets if you already know
them (this overrides the calculated ones), connect Apple Health (iPhone
only), turn on notifications, or paste in a recipe link to import right
away. None of these are required — you can skip past all of them.

**What happens next:** for most people today, a guided first log.

### 7. Log something, right now

Rather than dropping you straight onto an empty Today screen, Sloe walks
you through logging one real thing — a quick tap for breakfast, coffee, or
a search box — so your very first moment in the app is something you've
actually done, not a blank diary. After that, you may see a one-time, fully
skippable offer to start a free trial of Sloe Pro; skipping it takes you
straight to Today with no further detour.

**Why this step exists:** having something real logged, rather than just
being shown a number, makes your very first moment in the app feel useful
right away.

**What happens next:** Today — your home screen from here on. Your recipe
collection also starts out with a small set of recipes chosen to match your
diet and allergy answers, so you're never staring at an empty list on day
one.

## Signing back in

If you already have an account, use "I already have an account" from the
welcome screen (or open the app again — a returning signed-in device goes
straight to the tabs, no sign-in needed). If you've forgotten your password,
the sign-in screen has a "Forgot password?" link and a magic-link option
that emails you a one-tap sign-in link instead.

## The four tabs

Once you've finished setting up, Sloe's home is four tabs: **Today** (your
diary and calorie ring), **Plan** (build out your week from your library),
**Recipes** (your saved recipes, with a Discover feed for browsing
community recipes one tab over), and **Progress** (your trends, plus
account and settings). There's no separate "Discover" or "More" tab — both
live inside Recipes and Progress respectively.

## A note on your calorie ring

If you go over your target on a given day, the ring and numbers turn
**amber** — never red. Sloe deliberately doesn't use warning-red for
"you ate more than planned"; that's not a mistake you need to be shown as a
danger colour.

## A few things worth knowing

- **If your profile doesn't save at the end of the questions,** you'll stay
  on that screen with a message asking you to try again, rather than being
  sent on to Today with the wrong numbers.
- **If you get logged out partway through,** you're sent back to the
  account creation step, and your earlier answers are kept — you won't have
  to start over.
- **If your diet or allergy answers rule out a lot of recipes,** Sloe still
  tries to give you a starting set of recipes rather than leaving your
  collection empty.
- **If you're on the iPhone app and don't see your targets before you sign
  up,** as described in step 4 — that's expected for now, not a bug.

## What's next

- **Log your first meal (or your next one).** Search for a food, scan a
  barcode, use your voice, snap a photo, or use quick-add for the things
  you eat all the time.
- **Import a recipe from anywhere on the web.** Paste in a link, share it
  straight from Instagram or TikTok, or take a photo of a page from a
  cookbook.
